* **Victoria Belim‑Frolova (Bois de Jasmin / trained perfumer & evaluator) — “materials-first” selection \+ training protocol**  
  * **A professional *palette* is organized by functional role, not by “notes.”** Belim‑Frolova groups a foundational study set into **Foundations** (structure/clarity/diffusion), **Modifiers & Nuancers** (adds realism/modernity), and **Accents & Finishers** (“skin” \+ rounding). This is a *repeatable infrastructure* for turning a user’s language (“airy floral,” “clean musk,” “woody-amber backbone”) into *ingredient-level hypotheses* rather than vibe labels.  
  * **IFF training scale & the “small palette” argument:** she notes an IFF beginner kit at \~**360 core materials**, and relays an internal-school critique (Ellena) that this may be “too much,” motivating a **smaller, sharper palette** you can *actually learn*. This matters because recommendation quality depends on *stable internal anchors* (materials you can consistently recognize), not ever-expanding catalogs.  
  * **Dose control as a technical skill (not a trivia detail):**  
    * **Aldehyde C‑10 (decanal)**: “buy the smallest amount; study at **0.1% dilution**” → a concrete protocol for controlling intensity so a learner can perceive structure without overload.  
    * **Neroli**: “expensive; **5–10% dilution** is fine for study” → shows how cost/rarity changes *training* approach.  
    * **Restricted material substitution:** she flags **heliotropin (piperonal) restrictions** and suggests **Heliotropex** as a substitute—i.e., the system must handle *regulatory-driven substitutions* that reshape “the same” accord across brands/years.  
  * **“Classic moss” engineering under constraint:** she explicitly pairs **oakmoss absolute (or Evernyl)** in the woody/balsamic architecture list—an operational cue that modern “moss” effects often come from **molecular stand-ins**, changing how you diagnose “vintage chypre” vs “clean modern moss.”  
  * **A method to build *stable descriptors* (useful for consumer Q\&A):**  
    * “**Write three words** for each material” in a structured way: **(family classification) \+ (one general descriptor) \+ (one specific discriminator)**; her example claims bergamot has a “peppery nuance” that becomes a discrimination key once learned. This is a *compression algorithm* for user-facing Q\&A (force the model to extract discriminators, not adjectives).  
    * **Interval smelling schedule** (daily → every other day → twice/week → once/week) presented as the IFF pattern for long-term scent memory—i.e., recommendation systems should not only “predict,” but can also *teach* users to become more legible signalers over time.  
    * **Contrast-pair training:** “pair opposites” (citrus+balsamic, floral+woody, aromatic+musky) because “the brain remembers contrast faster than similarity.” That’s a direct design rule for Q\&A: ask contrast questions to quickly locate a user in scent-space.  
  * **Operational Q\&A template embedded in her recommendation practice:** in her recurring “Recommend Me a Perfume” threads, she instructs requesters to include **likes/dislikes, signature perfumes, and budget**, explicitly to get “better tailored” recommendations—this is a minimal input schema you can formalize into your system prompts/forms.  
  * **Evaluation logic that binds art \+ engineering:** her 5‑star rating definition (as reported) requires a fragrance be both emotionally satisfying *and* “**technically impeccably well‑crafted**.” For a recommender, that’s a split-criterion objective: preference match \+ craft/finish threshold.  
  * **Professional training context:** she frames her guidance as based on her own **IFF perfumery student** experience and explicitly points toward the **Jean Carles method** as a training backbone—useful as provenance for why her training protocol is “industry-shaped,” not hobbyist.  
* **Claire Vukcevic (Take One Thing Off) — “factor model” for natural-material variance (oud)**  
  * **Oud selection is multicausal, not a single-note preference.** Vukcevic enumerates the drivers of why oud can smell radically different: **geography/terroir & microclimate; species; cultivation method (wild vs plantation); inoculation method; age; how extensive the resin/rot is; wood preparation; distillation method; fermentation; oil separation method; and aging (force vs natural).** This is basically a *feature model* for interrogating “What kind of oud do you mean?” with non-vague follow-ups.  
  * **Terroir is treated as an actionable predictor, not romantic garnish.** She frames geography/microclimate effects as explanatory variables for consistent style differences—meaning a system can legitimately ask origin/style questions (region, production tradition) and produce causal justifications (“X style tends to… because production variables differ”).  
  * **Synthetic substitution is part of “authenticity matching.”** In discussing oud styles, she notes perfumers sometimes use a specific synthetic (e.g., a Firmenich material) to recreate a characteristic facet when naturals are scarce—this is crucial because consumers often ask for “that medicinal/barnyard oud,” and the best answer may be a *molecularly constructed* solution rather than a natural-origin one.  
* **Elena Vosnaki (Perfume Shrine) — historical taxonomy \+ “what’s actually doing the work” in structure**  
  * **Aldehydic florals are defined by a *dose/signature molecule set*, not by “has aldehydes.”**  
    * She distinguishes the trope as requiring a **significant amount of aliphatic aldehydes** (the **C10/C11/C12** “fatty aldehydes,” named by carbon count) combined with floral/woody/animalic structure.  
    * She gives a rare quantitative anchor: Chanel No. 5’s aldehydes are described as \~**1% of the formula**—a hard fact that explains why this genre reads as “abstract/perfumey/soapy” at a structural level, not a mood level.  
    * She ties “notes” to feasibility: e.g., lilac/carnation often require **reconstruction via combinations/synthetics** because the flowers yield insufficient essence—this is a direct warning against naïve “note literalism” in recommendation logic.  
  * **Chypre is a strict scaffold (a true accord), and she names the scaffold.**  
    * She defines the “basic structure” as an accord between **bergamot – oakmoss – labdanum**, and even pins geographic/biological provenance (oakmoss as a lichen; labdanum as cistus resin historically gathered from goats’ hair). This is causal grounding for why chypre reads as “citrus over mossy-amber depth.”  
    * She corrects a common historical simplification: **Coty 1917 wasn’t the first** to name a perfume “Chypre”; she cites **Guerlain Chypre de Paris (1909)** and **Chypre d’Orsay (1912)** as earlier naming examples. For a recommender, this matters because “chypre” is both a *family* and a *historical naming lineage* that affects consumer expectations.  
  * **Regulation-driven family drift (“nouveau chypres”):**  
    * She argues many modern perfumes branded “chypre” are technically **woody floral musks**, using **clean patchouli \+ vetiver** to stand in for a reduced oakmoss ratio due to allergen constraints; she connects this to **reformulations** that make classics “unrecognizable.” This is a mechanistic explanation for why “I love vintage Mitsouko but hate modern ‘chypres’” is common.  
  * **Perfume architecture is often synthetic “bones,” not a neat pyramid.**  
    * She attributes the “fragrance pyramid” pedagogical concept to **Jean Carles** as a way to explain structure to outsiders—then warns modern perfumes often don’t behave so neatly, and note pyramids are frequently marketing blueprints.  
    * She states structure is not just volatility sequencing; it’s the **roles \+ ratios** that create “the bones,” and notes synthetics are often used because they’re single molecules and stable.  
    * She provides a high-signal quantitative example: **Trésor uses \~21.4% Galaxolide**, and notes Ellena’s use of **Iso E Super** for structure/diffusion. For recommendation, these facts map “clean-warm musk cloud” and “woody-musky radiance” to plausible molecular drivers.  
  * **Skin chemistry: a marketing myth with a specific origin story.**  
    * She frames “personal chemistry” as “largely a myth” and explicitly traces a marketing use to **Chanel No. 5** after mass popularization (1950s), with messaging implying the perfume “becomes” the wearer. This is useful because consumers frequently self-diagnose mismatch as “my chemistry,” and the system needs a grounded alternative explanation (diffusion, dose, context, expectation).

## **Analysis of the Role of Information**

A perfume recommender fails when it treats preference as “liked notes” and ignores *why* the sensation occurs. The extracted anchors above provide **causal handles** and **repeatable selection infrastructure** that let the system justify recommendations like an evaluator rather than a copywriter. Edwards’ Wheel supplies a stable *coordinate system* (family neighborhoods) for exploration—so the model can move users through adjacent families instead of guessing randomly. Belim‑Frolova’s material‑role palette and training protocol turns vague user language into **ingredient-level discriminators** (e.g., “radiant floral” vs “powdery floral” → hedione/ionones/aldehydes; “mossy depth” → oakmoss/Evernyl/labdanum scaffolding), and her contrast-based elicitation rules suggest how to ask questions that separate nearby scents quickly. Vosnaki supplies the crucial bridge between marketing (“notes,” pyramids) and mechanics (dose, molecule sets, synthetic structural bones), including rare quantitative anchors (≈1% aldehydes in No.5; ≈21.4% Galaxolide in Trésor) that explain why two “clean floral” perfumes can feel totally different in diffusion and abstraction. Vukcevic’s oud factor model prevents a common failure mode—treating “oud” as one preference—by forcing the system to ask about provenance/process variables (terroir, cultivation, inoculation, distillation, aging) that actually drive the medicinal vs fruity vs smoky outcomes consumers mean. Behnke’s concentration realism protects the model from overpromising (“just make it stronger”) by encoding that performance changes can distort accords (especially bright citrus) and that structural performance often comes from musks/woody-ambers, not just more oil. Finally, Alavi’s structured “Twenty Blotters” logic and his regulatory-economic framing help the system handle real-world constraints: reformulations, safety restrictions, and availability—so recommendations remain feasible and explanations remain credible.

## **Foundational Concepts (Glossary)**

**Accord**  
A deliberately engineered combination of materials that is perceived as a coherent unit (often more important than any single ingredient). Accords define “scaffolds” for families (e.g., chypre accord) and are the correct object for matching when users say “I like this *style*.”

**Aldehydic Floral**  
A subcategory of floral perfumes characterized by a **prominent dose-signature** of aliphatic aldehydes (commonly C10/C11/C12) integrated with floral/woody/animalic structure, yielding an abstract, often “soapy/perfumey” effect.

**Aliphatic Aldehydes (C10/C11/C12)**  
A set of “fatty” aldehydes (named by carbon count) used to create lift, sparkle, waxy-clean diffusion, and an abstracted “perfumey” aura. They act as *structural modifiers*—small changes in dose can flip a fragrance from “fresh floral” to “classic aldehydic.”

**Ambroxan**  
A widely used ambergris-type molecule that provides woody-amber diffusion, tenacity, and a “mineral-amber” backbone. In Q\&A, strong Ambroxan signatures often correlate with “clean, long-lasting, woody-amber skin aura.”

**Attar**  
A concentrated perfume oil—often alcohol-free—traditionally produced by distillation of botanicals (e.g., rose, sandalwood, oud) and sometimes captured into a base oil (historically sandalwood). For recommendation, attars behave differently in diffusion and wear (oil vs alcohol, different volatility curve).

**Blotter**  
An absorbent paper strip used to evaluate fragrance off-skin. In structured elicitation (e.g., “Twenty Blotters”), it becomes a tool for *standardizing comparison* so answers are less confounded by skin effects.

**Chypre**  
A family defined by a **strict structural accord**: bergamot (citrus opening) \+ oakmoss (mossy depth) \+ labdanum (amber-resin body). Modern “chypres” may drift if oakmoss is reduced/substituted.

**Civet / Civettone**  
Civet is a classic animalic note historically from animal secretion; modern perfumery commonly uses molecules like civettone to recreate warm, musky-animalic effects. In Q\&A, “animalic warmth” can be satisfied via these reconstructions without animal sourcing.

**Diffusion**  
How scent radiates into the air around the wearer (distinct from longevity). Diffusion is heavily influenced by certain musks and woody-amber molecules (e.g., Iso E Super) and by overall formula architecture.

**Distillation Method (Hydrodistillation vs Steam Distillation)**  
A production choice that changes which molecules make it into the final oil and in what ratios. Hydrodistillation involves plant material immersed in water; steam distillation passes steam through material. For naturals (especially oud/attars), method differences can change smokiness, sweetness, sharpness, and off-notes.

**Evernyl**  
A synthetic material used to approximate key facets of oakmoss (mossy-woody “chypre” depth). It matters because many “mossy” modern fragrances achieve the effect via substitutes rather than high oakmoss content.

**Fermentation (in natural oils like oud)**  
Microbial/enzymatic transformations during soaking/aging/prep that alter odor-active compounds. In Q\&A, “fermented/barnyard/cheesy/animalic” facets often trace to fermentation/rot extent and subsequent processing choices.

**Fixation**  
The capacity of a perfume to “hold together” and evaporate slowly/steadily over time. Achieved through material choice (resins, woods, musks) and architecture (ratios, interactions), not merely higher concentration.

**Flanker**  
A version/variant of an existing perfume released under the same name lineage (often with a modifier) that preserves recognizable DNA while altering one or more structural elements (sweetness, freshness, concentration, key accord emphasis). For recommendation, flankers require “what changed?” logic, not just “same brand.”

**Fragrance Families**  
Taxonomic groupings (e.g., Amber, Mossy Woods, Aromatic Fougère) that cluster perfumes by shared structural signatures. Families enable systematic exploration: identify a user’s home family, then test adjacent families to refine taste.

**Fragrance Wheel**  
A structured map of fragrance families designed to model relationships between “innate preferences” and family groupings. In recommendation, it functions like a navigation graph: you can move locally (adjacent exploration) instead of jumping across the scent-space.

**Fougère**  
A classic structure built around lavender \+ coumarin-like sweetness \+ mossy/woody support, often with aromatic herbs and clean woods. In Q\&A, “barbershop aromatic freshness” often points here.

**Galaxolide**  
A “clean/warm” synthetic musk widely used to build laundry-clean radiance and long-lasting musky volume. High use levels can define the entire diffusion profile of a fragrance.

**Hedione**  
A jasmine-related molecule used to add airy radiance, lift, and diffusion—often responsible for “transparent floral glow” rather than dense floral sweetness.

**IFRA**  
International Fragrance Association; provides safety standards that influence what materials can be used and at what levels. Recommendation systems must account for IFRA-driven reformulations, substitutions, and the economic burden of compliance (especially for small brands).

**Inoculation (Agarwood/Oud Production)**  
The intentional process of infecting/stimulating Aquilaria trees to produce resinous agarwood. Different inoculation methods can change resin character, affecting the resulting oud oil style.

**Iso E Super**  
A woody-amber molecule often used to create smooth woody radiance, volume, and diffusion—frequently experienced as “clean woods” or “transparent cedar/amber.” Notably used as a structural tool in modern perfumery.

**Labdanum (Resinoid)**  
A resinous material from cistus (rockrose) used to create amber depth and warmth; a core component of chypre and amber structures.

**Microclimate / Terroir (Perfumery Context)**  
Local environmental conditions (temperature, humidity, soil, ecosystem) affecting plant/wood chemistry and thus aroma profile. In Q\&A, terroir is how you justify origin-linked differences (e.g., two ouds or two vetivers smelling meaningfully different).

**Oakmoss**  
A lichen-derived material historically central to chypres, providing mossy, bitter-green, forest depth. Allergen concerns and regulation can reduce usage, leading to reformulation and increased reliance on substitutes.

**Oud (Agarwood Oil)**  
An oil distilled from resinous agarwood (formed when Aquilaria is infected/stressed). Oud is not a single smell: its profile depends on species, terroir, cultivation, infection extent, distillation, separation, and aging choices.

**Sillage**  
The perceptible “trail” a perfume leaves as the wearer moves. It emerges from diffusion \+ volatility \+ dose \+ environment; it is not synonymous with “strong” or “long-lasting.”

**Structure**  
The engineered architecture of a perfume—how materials and ratios create coherence, transitions, and persistence. Modern structure often relies on stable single-molecule synthetics (“bones”), and the “fragrance pyramid” is best treated as a teaching metaphor rather than a universal model.

**“Nouveau Chypre”**  
A modern category label for perfumes marketed as chypre but structurally closer to woody-floral-musk compositions, often compensating for reduced oakmoss with cleaner patchouli/vetiver bases.

**“Twenty Blotters” (Elicitation Format)**  
A fixed-question, quick-answer interview format used to reveal preferences and constraints efficiently. As a system design pattern, it suggests using standardized prompts to extract comparable preference vectors across users.

# **Cognitive Alignment — Distractor Mitigation**

## **Direct Contradiction Pairs**

* **\[Misconception\]**: “Notes” are literal ingredients; if it’s listed, it’s *in the bottle.*  
  **\[Expert Correction (Vosnaki)\]**: Many “notes” are **effects** or **reconstructions** (especially for materials that don’t yield usable essences at scale). Treat note lists as *communication/marketing*, not ingredient disclosure.  
* **\[Misconception\]**: The note pyramid (top/middle/base) is a universal technical model of how perfume truly wears.  
  **\[Expert Correction (Vosnaki)\]**: The pyramid is primarily a **teaching metaphor** (often attributed in this context to Jean Carles) and modern perfumes frequently don’t behave as a neat three-act volatility story; pyramids are commonly **marketing blueprints** rather than structural truth.  
* **\[Misconception\]**: “Aldehydic” just means “soapy/clean,” or “it contains some aldehydes somewhere.”  
  **\[Expert Correction (Vosnaki)\]**: Aldehydic florals are defined by **prominent structural use** of aliphatic aldehydes (C10/C11/C12 family), not by a vibe label. She anchors the genre with a concrete reference point: Chanel No. 5’s aldehydes described as \~**1%** of the formula (dose-signature thinking).  
* **\[Misconception\]**: If something is labeled “Chypre,” it’s structurally a chypre.  
  **\[Expert Correction (Vosnaki)\]**: Chypre is a **specific engineered scaffold/accord** built around **bergamot \+ oakmoss \+ labdanum** (a structure), not a generic “mossy perfume” name.  
* **\[Misconception\]**: Modern “chypres” are the same thing as classic chypres.  
  **\[Expert Correction (Vosnaki)\]**: Many modern “nouveau chypres” drift into **woody floral musks**, relying more on **clean patchouli \+ vetiver** and “moss effects” because of allergen/regulatory constraints and reformulations; family names can remain while molecular drivers change.  
* **\[Misconception\]**: “Mossy” implies lots of real oakmoss.  
  **\[Expert Correction (Belim‑Frolova \+ Vosnaki)\]**: “Moss” is often achieved via **substitutes/stand-ins** (e.g., Evernyl-type moss effects) or architecture shifts under restrictions; “mossy” should be treated as an **engineered effect** unless verified.  
* **\[Misconception\]**: “Oud” is a single note; if it says oud, it will smell like “oud,” and it likely contains real oud oil.  
  **\[Expert Correction (Vukcevic)\]**: Oud character is **multi-causal**: terroir/microclimate, species, wild vs plantation, inoculation method, resin/rot extent, wood prep, distillation, fermentation, separation, and aging all change the odor profile. Marketing “oud” ≠ a predictable, single smell.  
* **\[Misconception\]**: Naturals are inherently superior/more authentic; synthetics are inferior “chemicals.”  
  **\[Expert Correction (Vosnaki \+ Belim‑Frolova \+ Vukcevic)\]**: Modern perfume structure frequently uses synthetics as stable **“bones”** (single-molecule reliability and predictable diffusion/tenacity). Restrictions and cost make substitutions normal engineering (not “fake”), and synthetics may be intentionally used to recreate specific facets when naturals vary or are scarce.  
* **\[Misconception\]**: “Personal skin chemistry” is the main cause of mismatch; it’s mystical and unavoidable.  
  **\[Expert Correction (Vosnaki)\]**: “Skin chemistry” is framed as largely a **marketing myth**, popularized in messaging (notably around Chanel No. 5\) implying the perfume “becomes” the wearer. Technical explanations should prioritize diffusion, dose, architecture, and context.  
* **\[Misconception\]**: Higher concentration (EDP/extrait) automatically improves longevity/sillage without changing the smell.  
  **\[Expert Correction (Behnke)\]**: You can’t just “turn up” oil content: pushing materials can **distort** the accord (he specifically warns using bergamot as an example), so “stronger” can mean “different/worse,” not “better performance.”  
* **\[Misconception\]**: Market labels (“niche,” “luxury”) are reliable predictors of structure and quality.  
  **\[Expert Correction (Alavi)\]**: “Niche” is treated as overcrowded/overused; it’s weak as a predictive feature. Use structure, families, and engineered drivers rather than market category labels.  
* **\[Misconception\]**: Classification is subjective; it can’t be systematized for selection.  
  **\[Expert Correction (Edwards)\]**: The Fragrance Wheel is explicit **selection infrastructure** linking preference patterns to family neighborhoods—enabling systematic “what to sample next” logic instead of vibes.

## **Explicit “Verify & Rectify” Rules**

Use these as imperative Cognitive Nexus rules (trigger → verify → rectify). Each rule is designed to block the most common marketing-term hallucinations.

1. **Note-to-Accord Translation Rule**  
   If the user mentions a **note**, the system **MUST** translate it into an **effect hypothesis** (accord/family/structural driver) before recommending. Do **not** assert literal ingredient presence from a note list.  
2. **Pyramid Skepticism Rule**  
   Treat note pyramids **only** as loose communication aids. The system **MUST** prioritize structural drivers (accord scaffold \+ diffusion/tenacity materials) for prediction and explanation.  
3. **Accord-First Rule for Classic Families**  
   If a user requests a named classical family (especially **chypre**), the system **MUST** verify the defining scaffold (e.g., **bergamot–oakmoss–labdanum**) before validating the match. If verification is not possible, **label the recommendation as an interpretation** (e.g., “modern chypre-style / woody-floral-musk”).  
4. **Historical Drift Disclosure Rule**  
   When a family name has known drift (e.g., “nouveau chypres”), the system **MUST** disclose that modern examples may be structurally different due to reformulation/regulatory constraints, and **MUST** ask whether the user wants “classic scaffold” or “modern interpretation.”  
5. **Moss Verification Rule**  
   If the user asks for “mossy/oakmoss/chypre depth,” the system **MUST** distinguish:  
   * true oakmoss-driven effects vs  
   * substitute-driven moss effects (Evernyl-type) vs  
   * patchouli/vetiver “clean moss” impressions.  
     Then recommend accordingly, explicitly naming which route is used.  
6. **Skin Chemistry Myth Filter**  
   If the user blames “my chemistry,” the system **MUST NOT** validate mystical chemistry as the primary cause. The system **MUST** test technical causes first: dose, application points, diffusion architecture, and context/expectation mismatch.  
7. **Natural-vs-Synthetic Neutrality Rule**  
   The system **MUST NOT** rank perfumes as better/worse based on “natural” alone. The system **MUST** treat synthetics as legitimate structural tools (“bones”) and treat substitutions as normal engineering under restrictions/availability.  
8. **Oud Disambiguation Rule**  
   If the user says “oud,” the system **MUST** verify which they mean:  
   * a natural-material outcome (style/type) or  
   * a constructed “oud accord.”  
     The system **MUST** ask at least one factor-model question (style preference) grounded in production variables (e.g., medicinal vs fruity vs smoky; clean vs animalic).  
9. **Performance ≠ Concentration Rule**  
   The system **MUST NOT** promise that moving to EDP/extrait automatically improves longevity/sillage. The system **MUST** warn that increasing load can **distort** accords (bergamot cited as an example) and must instead seek structural performance drivers when the user wants projection/tenacity.  
10. **Taxonomy-as-Infrastructure Rule**  
    The system **MUST** use a stable family map (Wheel-style neighborhood logic) to guide exploration and avoid “vibe hopping.” Recommendations should state the **family zone** and the **structural reason** it fits.  
11. **Market-Label Deweighting Rule**  
    The system **MUST** treat labels like “niche” as low-signal. Use family/accord/diffusion drivers as the primary similarity basis.  
12. **Unverified-Claim Suppression Rule**  
    If you cannot verify the underlying scaffold/material reality, you **MUST** downgrade certainty and speak in effect-language (“moss effect,” “oud-style accord,” “aldehydic lift”) rather than inventing ingredients, naturals, or exact composition claims.

## **Ambiguity Shields**

These are high-noise terms that routinely cause wrong matches. The system should treat them as **ambiguous entities** and force disambiguation before recommending.

* **“Oud”**  
  * **Natural Oud Oil**: distilled resinous agarwood outcome that varies by terroir/process.  
  * **Oud Accord**: a constructed effect (often using synthetics to target specific facets).  
    **Shield**: Ask “Do you mean natural-material character (medicinal/smoky/animalic/fruity), or an oud-style woody accord?”  
* **“Chypre”**  
  * **Classic Chypre Accord**: bergamot–oakmoss–labdanum scaffold.  
  * **Nouveau Chypre Label**: modern woody-floral-musk interpretations, often patchouli/vetiver driven.  
    **Shield**: Ask “classic scaffold vs modern interpretation?”  
* **“Mossy” / “Oakmoss”**  
  * **Oakmoss-driven** vs **substitute-driven moss effect (Evernyl-type)** vs **clean patchouli/vetiver ‘moss’ impression**.  
    **Shield**: Ask whether they want bitter-green foresty depth or a cleaner modern “moss” base.  
* **“Aldehydic”**  
  * **Dose-signature aldehydic floral** (C10/C11/C12 prominence) vs “smells soapy/clean.”  
    **Shield**: Ask whether they like the *abstract sparkling* “classic aldehydic” lift or just “clean laundry.”  
* **“Natural” / “Clean” / “Authentic”**  
  * Can mean ingredient ideology, or can mean a smell effect (fresh/airy/low-sweet).  
    **Shield**: Force the user to specify: “Do you mean *materials provenance*, *IFRA/allergen sensitivity*, or *the smell profile*?” Then proceed with structure-first reasoning.  
* **“My chemistry”**  
  * Often a narrative placeholder for diffusion, dosing, and expectation mismatch.  
    **Shield**: Redirect to controlled tests (application amount/location, environment, comparing blotter vs skin) before attributing causality.  
* **“Beast mode” / “strong”**  
  * Could mean projection, sillage, longevity, or just intensity.  
    **Shield**: Require the user to choose which axis matters, and warn that “more concentration” can distort accords.  
* **“Niche”**  
  * Often a market/social label, not a structural descriptor.  
    **Shield**: Treat it as a buying-context tag only (price/availability), not a smell predictor.
